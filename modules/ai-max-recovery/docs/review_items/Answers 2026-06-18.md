## Cross-campaign checks: yes, necessary, here's why

Your assumption, "different campaigns target different things, so a negative in one can't damage another." That's true at the campaign-design intent level. It's false at the match-execution level, which is where Google Ads actually operates and where conflicts happen.

Three reasons cross-campaign checks are non-negotiable:

**Match types create cross-campaign collisions even between unrelated topics.** Phrase and broad match keywords don't respect campaign boundaries. A campaign with "dental services" on phrase match will match "best dental services london." A separate campaign with "service providers london" on phrase match will match the same query. If you then add "services" as a negative in the dental campaign, it can also affect the "service providers" campaign depending on whether the negative is at campaign level, ad-group level, or account level (shared list).

**The brand-spend leak we're trying to detect IS the cross-campaign problem.** One of the Reddit pain point quotes I use in my poster campaign is "... the client has no idea they're spending so much on their own brand". This happens precisely because non-brand campaigns leak into brand territory. The PPC manager has separate brand and non-brand campaigns, and the non-brand campaign's broad match catches branded queries. Without cross-campaign checking, you can't detect this situation at all. The conflict-detection moat goes away. We become a negative keyword classifier with no edge over a Claude skill prompt.

**Shared negative lists are standard practice.** Agencies often maintain account-level negative lists ("never bid on these terms anywhere"). When a user adds a negative through SearchSavior, they may add it at campaign, ad-group, or account level. The system has to check positives across all campaigns to flag what that decision will damage. The dental/plumbing example only works if every negative is added at campaign level only, and even then shared lists exist as a separate mechanism.

If you want to reduce cost on the LLM call, the smarter cut is: only check positives that COULD collide based on word overlap with the proposed negative, rather than every positive in the account. That's a performance refinement. Keeps the feature itself intact.

## Where to put competitor info

Separate field. Two reasons:

**Structured input enables constrained generation.** If competitor brands is its own dedicated list, the prompt can say "only classify as competitor-brand if the term contains a string from this list." That's how we kill the "master" hallucination at source. Merged into business context as free text, the LLM would have to infer which words are competitors from natural language, which is the exact hallucination problem we're trying to solve.

**User mental model is cleaner with separation.** "My brand" / "competitor brands" / "general business info" are three distinct things the user thinks about separately. A merged field is confusing UX and harder to update. So this would be 3 distinct input fields in 'Settings'.

## What else to give as input

Most useful additions, in priority order:

1. **Active positive keywords across all campaigns, with match types.** This is the conflict-detection foundation. Without it, the LLM can't reason about what a recommended negative will damage. Even if our conflict checker runs deterministically downstream, the LLM's recommendations should be informed by what's actually being bid on. This is the 'check-back-against'-list.

2. **Existing negative keywords list (campaign-level, ad-group-level, and shared lists).** Stops the LLM from recommending duplicates or terms the user already removed manually.

3. **Own brand names list (already in user_settings.brand_names).** The LLM should know not to flag own-brand queries as toxic.

4. **Conversion data per term (cost, conversions, conversion value).** Lets the LLM distinguish expensive zero-converters from expensive high-converters. We may already have cost and conversions, but worth checking conversion value is in the payload.

5. **Geographic context.** Especially for AI Max which uses geo signals heavily. Local-service campaigns get misjudged without it.

Lower priority but worth noting for later:

6. **User's prior reversal actions.** If a user previously unblocked a recommendation, that's a signal it was wrong. Feeds back into recommendation quality over time.

7. **Campaign objective (lead gen, e-commerce, awareness).** Changes what "toxic" means.

I'd ship v1 with items 1 to 4. Items 5 to 7 are v2 once we see how the v1 outputs perform in the wild.
